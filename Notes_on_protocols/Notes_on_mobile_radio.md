## Two Ways of Sharing the Spectrum: FDD (frequency division duplexing) & TDD (time division duplexing)

![FDD_vs_TDD](http://github.com/sergiocollado/potpourri/blob/master/Notes_on_protocols/Images_mobile_communication/FDD_vs_TDD.png)


There are to two distinct variants ‚Äčof wireless communication called FDD and TDD, ‚Äčwhich respectively stand for ‚Äčfrequency division duplexing and time division duplexing. ‚ÄčAt its heart, they are simply ‚Äčtwo ways to share the limited and expensive spectrum. ‚ÄčBut in this particular context, ‚Äčthey aren't necessarily the way to share ‚Äčthe spectrum among other users on the phone. ‚ÄčRather, they are other ways to share spectrum ‚Äčbetween the cell tower or ‚Äčthe base station and the rest of the phones.

‚ÄčLet's look at what those are one-by-one at a high level. ‚ÄčFDD, once again, ‚Äčit stands for **frequency division duplexing**, ‚Äčentails and requires rather a pair of ‚Äčradio channels for simultaneous two-way communication. ‚ÄčFor example, if you have abundant frequency spectrum, ‚Äčyou could allocate one channel ‚Äčon which your cell tower would transmit. ‚ÄčYou could allocate ‚Äča different independent frequency channel ‚Äčon which your phone would transmit. ‚ÄčIn technical parlance, now ‚Äčthe transmissions that are sent by the base station or ‚Äčthe cell tower are called downlink and ‚Äčthe transmissions that are sent by ‚Äčyour phone are called uplink. ‚ÄčIt is called so because ‚Äčtraditionally base station is located at a larger height, ‚Äčit is taller than normal users, ‚Äčand hence, the signal from ‚Äčthe base station comes down to you. ‚ÄčWhereas, when your phone transmits a signal to ‚Äča base station because base station ‚Äčis located at a higher level, ‚Äčthe phone signal has to travel upwards, so to speak. 
‚ÄčThat's why the name downlink, ‚Äčwhereas this is uplink. ‚ÄčIn FDD, your system is rich ‚Äčenough to buy two separate channels. ‚ÄčOne channel, wherein only the base station will ‚Äčtransmit and the phone will only receive on the channel. ‚ÄčWhereas on the second uplink channel, ‚Äčit is only the phone that is allowed to transmit, ‚Äčand the base station will ‚Äčonly need to receive on that channel. ‚ÄčThere is separation of responsibilities ‚Äčin the frequency domain, so to speak. ‚ÄčThere are, as always, pros and cons. ‚ÄčThe pros are that, ‚Äčas the first point mentions here, ‚Äčbecause those two are independent channels, ‚Äčit allows for simultaneous two-way communications. 

‚ÄčIn that the base station can ‚Äčtransmit something to the phone on the downlink. ‚ÄčAt the same time while the phone is listening, ‚Äčit can also try to send ‚Äčsome information to the same base station on the uplink, ‚Äčthus allow for two-way communication. ‚ÄčHowever, the basic disadvantage of ‚Äčthis FDD mechanism is that you have to ‚Äčhave enough budget in order to ‚Äčbuy two separate frequency channels. ‚ÄčNot only that, your local ‚Äčregulatory authorities have to have ‚Äčabundance spectrum available in order to ‚Äčallocate two independent channels for your system. ‚ÄčOne for downlink, one for uplink. ‚ÄčBut regardless of those pros and cons, ‚Äčone fundamental factor about FDD ‚Äčis what attribute of the frequency channel it leverages. ‚ÄčKeep in mind, I mentioned that multiple access is ‚Äčabout leveraging certain attributes ‚Äčof the wireless channel. 

‚ÄčIn case of FDD, ‚Äčit goes without saying that it leverages ‚Äčthe frequency attribute of the channel in that it ‚Äčseparates the uplink and downlink transmissions in ‚Äčfrequency by making those go on two different channels. ‚ÄčThat is the fundamental premise of FDD. ‚ÄčThe other side of the coin would be TDD, ‚Äčalso known as **time division duplexing**. ‚ÄčAs you may have guessed, ‚Äčit is called so because it leverages ‚Äčthe time aspect of the wireless channel. ‚ÄčThe hallmark of a TDD system is that, ‚Äčunlike an FDD system, ‚Äča TDD system has access to only one frequency channel. ‚ÄčThere are no two frequency channels anymore. ‚ÄčBoth the base station and ‚Äčyour phones have to ‚Äčoperate on the same frequency channel. 

‚ÄčSingle radio channel is ‚Äčthe basic characteristic of a TDD system. ‚ÄčBut if you have to accommodate ‚Äčtransmissions in two different ‚Äčdirections on the same channel, ‚Äčhow can you make that happen? ‚ÄčWell, that is where leveraging ‚Äčthe time attribute of the channel comes into picture. ‚ÄčFor example, on the same channel, ‚Äčyou might have the base station ‚Äčtransmits from, let's say, ‚Äčtime t_1 to t_2. ‚ÄčFrom time t_2 to t_3, ‚Äčyou will open that channel up for ‚Äčthe other direction of transmission, that is uplink. ‚ÄčOnce again, if the operating ‚Äčdata demand has been satisfied, ‚Äčyou can open the same channel back ‚Äčto the base station from t_3 to t_4. ‚ÄčOnce again, from t_4 to t_5, ‚Äčyou can let the phone transmit. 

‚ÄčBecause you have just one frequency channel, ‚Äčyou effectively have to ‚Äčalternate between the downlink and uplink ‚Äčon the base station transmissions and ‚Äčthe transmission by your phone. ‚ÄčThe pros and cons of TDD are ‚Äčthe exact counterparts of those for FDD. ‚ÄčIn that because you have to have ‚Äčonly one channel, a TDD system, ‚Äčgiven everything else equal, ‚Äčwill be cheaper to implement ‚Äčas compared to an FDD system. ‚ÄčBut because both downlink ‚Äčand uplink are looking to use the same channel, ‚Äčthe peak throughput or data rate that you can ‚Äčachieve in a TDD system is by definition, ‚Äčlimited as compared to an FDD system. ‚ÄčOnce again, given everything else is equal. ‚ÄčAs with any other engineering problems, ‚ÄčFDD and TDD are two ways ‚Äčto leverage that trade-off between ‚Äčthe cost of deploying the system ‚Äčversus the performance that the system will achieve.
